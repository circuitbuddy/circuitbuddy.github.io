The whole cycle in four moments

01 · SNAPQ1 switches on. Its collector — the top of the transistor — drops from 6 V to nearly 0 V. Capacitor C1 was charged up, and a capacitor cannot change its voltage instantly, so it drags Q2's base down with it, to about −4.6 V. Q2 is forced off and LED 2 goes dark, while current now flows freely through LED 1 and Q1.

02 · RECHARGEThe timer runs. C1 slowly recharges through the 47 kΩ resistor. Q2's base voltage climbs from −4.6 V toward +6 V — but it never gets there, because…

03 · TRIGGER…at +0.7 V, Q2 wakes up. The moment Q2's base crosses the threshold, Q2 switches on and its collector crashes to 0 V. Now capacitor C2 delivers the same dirty trick in reverse: it yanks Q1's base down to −4.6 V, knocking Q1 off. LED 1 dies, LED 2 lights.

04 · REPEATThe mirror image plays out. C2 recharges through the other 47 kΩ resistor until Q1's base hits +0.7 V again — flip, and we are back at step 01. Each half of the blink lasts about 0.7 × R × C = 0.7 × 47 000 × 0.00001 ≈ 0.33 seconds with these parts. (The animation runs slower than real life so you can watch it think.) Want a slower blink? Use bigger capacitors or bigger base resistors.
